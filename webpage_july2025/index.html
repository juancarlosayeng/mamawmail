Vertical & Horizontal Singularities


Horizontal Singularity Summary

In MAMAWMAIL, fractal propagation means each device sends a message to 3 unvisited devices per hop. A hop can happen up to a depth of 4. This ensures wide distribution while avoiding cycles or redundancy.

We want to identify how many unique message paths can be discovered from every device to all others, forming a saturated routing table. This includes multi-hop paths that traverse only unvisited devices at each hop.

The saturation point is reached when all pairs \((i, j)\) have at least one known path traversed through this fractal propagation rule:

\[ \text{Total paths needed} \geq \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j=1, j \neq i}^{N} \text{Paths}_{i \rightarrow j}\quad \text{(all-hop, unvisited)} \]

Vertical Singularity Summary

Vertical singularity refers to how many distinct message packets a single device must send to ensure successful delivery to all other devices â€” even if 90% of the swarm goes offline.

Unlike the horizontal case (which maps all paths between all pairs), this focuses on resilience by redundancy for a single sender (Device 0). The goal is to ensure every destination has multiple distinct paths that do not share critical middle relays.

\[ \text{Vertical Singularity: } \quad M \geq T \times R \]

Where:

  • M = Total Messages Sent
  • T = Number of Target Devices
  • R = Desired Redundant Paths per Target

For a swarm of 10 devices, with 9 targets and a goal of 10 distinct paths per target, the sender must dispatch 90 message packets. This creates enough redundancy to tolerate massive node loss, ensuring delivery paths still exist even if 90% of the swarm is offline.
